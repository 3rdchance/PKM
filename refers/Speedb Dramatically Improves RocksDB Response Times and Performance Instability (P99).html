Introduction to IOPS, Response Time and P99

When publishing performance of a storage system, the focus is typically on maximum IOPS (Input + Output Per Second) or throughput (MB/sec) while latency is often neglected. On the other hand, in many applications this latency aspect and especially the stability of the latency is the key factor in improvement of the performance. In the many cases where the maximum i/o can not be achieved, improving the latency will lead to better application performance.

For example, you can add lanes to a road to provide parallelism for more cars driving to a certain location, but this says nothing about the time it takes to get there or return. So the number of cars driving in parallel lanes in this example is the IOPS vs the time it takes to arrive, which would be the Latency and/or Response Time.

Let’s break it down:

IOPS, Latency and Response Time:

  1. IOPS quantifies how many read or write operations a storage device can perform in one second.
  2. Latency / Response Time refers to the total time it takes for a request to be processed and for the corresponding response to be received and ready for use by the requester.

In measuring response time there are two important additional markers to observe:

  1. The overall AVERAGE RESPONSE TIME  itself

# Formula for Average Response Time (AR): AR = (Sum of all response times) / (Number of data points)

  1. P99 which measures the STABILITY of the RESPONSE TIME  

# Formula for P99 Response Time: P99 = Value at (99th percentile position)

In our example of cars, the AVERAGE RESPONSE TIME is how long it takes the average car to make a round-trip. P99 would alternatively measure the slowest 1% of cars. So traffic jams during the rush hour for example may have a smaller impact on the average but will have a huge impact on the P99. 

Understanding P99 In Detail

The ‘P’ = Percentile, and the ‘99’ is the percentile being referenced. 

P99 represents the upper boundary of response times for read operations, indicating that 99% of queries should be completed within this threshold. This means 99% of requests will be faster than the given latency number desired. 

Put differently, only 1% of the requests will be slower than your P99 latency target, which makes it useful for resource and performance planning

Where and Why P99 Matters

If your application does random read and write operations that are independent by nature, you probably (usually) only care about the overall AVERAGE RESPONSE TIMES. 

However, when the application runs a list of (sequentially) dependent requests one after the other, or uses large batch operations, having guarantees on the 99th percentile at a certain latency (response time) is critical for the performance of overall completion of operations and effective net performance of the database or application. 

The other place where P99 will harm your performance is if your application gives 100% of performance as long as the response time is below a certain threshold. 

In some cases, overall average latency isn’t a good enough measurement, where P99 gives us better insights. 

RocksDB read Performance

Random read performance on traditional key-value-storage engines is practically the same in most implementations of any key-value store when the application has a significant portion of the data on SSD and the index fits into the RAM. The number of requests per second and the response time is highly dependent only on the underlying SSD. 

RocksDB generally follows this rule and exhibits excellent read performance, IF ONLY reads (and no writes) are being processed. By utilizing techniques such as bloom-filter or similar approaches, the number of false positives can be nearly eliminated, resulting in just 1 SSD read per request, thus as high efficiency is theoretically possible. The index size is very small compared to other approaches, which may lead to a better cache hit, and the fact that the data is semi-sorted allows you to also get very good performance in range queries.

RocksDB Write Performance

Due to optimization for small object writes, RocksDB is typically most popular in state store, metadata, caching, indexing and other such applications supporting databases (like Redis on Flash), applications and event streaming platforms like Kafka Streams, Apache Flink and Apache Spark.

Rocksdb write performance suffers when the database size exceeds over 50GB. In this case the write amplification may reach a very large number, which means that the system needs to do many reads and writes for each application write. 

RocksDB Read Performance Suffers Under Mixed Workload Conditions

One of the weakest points of RocksDB is the response time of mixed workload when the database is larger than the RAM of the particular (local) system. In this case both the average response time as well as the P99 numbers are significantly higher than the underlined potential SSD capabilities, especially in sharded environments where many shards share the same SSD.  

The issue of unstable performance is considered (by many) as an integral and inherent part of using the LSM technology. The system performs background operations, like compaction and flushes in a relatively large quanta and during this time the load on the underlying storage device(s) increases and the response times trends up. 

In a sharded environment, when there are many shards working on the same SSD,  the problem is even worse. For example, a few databases may start their background jobs at the same time and increase the load across the devices and hence increase the response time across all related workloads.  

However, RocksDB encounters challenges in scenarios where the workload consists of a combination of read and write operations, which is the common type of use-case. 

Even with a relatively low write rate (10%), the response time surpasses that of the SSD, leading to significantly higher average response time and P99 measurements. This issue becomes more pronounced in systems employing sharding across multiple instances.

LSM trees Are Not Inherently Limited on Read Performance or Related Stability

The myth that LSM trees are inherently limited in delivering high-performance reads and stable P99 has stifled motivated research and innovations in the past, as it’s been assumed that key-value storage solutions have likely reached near peak development. 

We will show evidence here with some practical examples, where applying Speedb’s specific developmental innovations, resource management and QoS controls can alleviate read-performance instability issues, and finally shatter this prevailing myth that LSM trees are inherently limited with read latency performance. 

Sharding Doesn’t Resolve P99 Challenges in RocksDB

Sharding in RocksDB, or any other database system, may not directly improve P99 performance because P99 is primarily influenced by the latency of individual operations within each shard, rather than the overall data partitioning strategy.

Even if the workload is distributed across multiple shards, certain individual operations within each shard might still experience higher latencies, leading to a higher P99 values.
In the context of RocksDB, sharding refers to the technique of horizontally partitioning the data across multiple independent instances (shards) of the RocksDB database. Sharding is typically deployed as best practice for 2 reasons: 

  1. Limited capacity is required per node  (i.e. as smallish State Store)
  2. RocksDB average latency and IOPS degrade at some particular capacity

First RocksDB Challenge Solved:

Reducing RocksDB WAF (Write Amplification Factor)

Speedb's initial enterprise release successfully reduced the huge WAF (write amplification factor) in RocksDB by about 80% and improved it significantly. This innovation called Multi-Dimensional Adaptive Compaction has a significantly positive impact on extracting maximum IOPS from SSDs as Speedb reduces automatic rewrites of entire levels during compaction runs. 

Speedb's strategic rewrite of the LSM tree frees up significant I/Os available from SSD which were not being used efficiently, thus having positive effect on latency and overall IOPS available.

Second RocksDB Challenge Solved:

Speedb’s Multifarious Enhancements Stabilize Read Performance and P99 Measurements

  • Various low-level algorithm and feature improvements
  • Enhanced our own Multi-Dimensional Adaptive Compaction routines
  • Dynamically measure the compaction pressure and prioritize the compaction based on the pressure. 
  • Adapt to the workload and assign dynamic QoS controls to the flush and compactions 
  • Compaction scheduler, currently running on each instance.

In RocksDB (and LSM tree(s) in general), data is organized into multiple levels, and over time, as data is constantly being written and deleted, these levels can become fragmented. Compaction is the process of merging and reorganizing data to improve efficiency and optimize storage.

The term "compaction pressure" is used to describe the extent to which the system is burdened or strained by the ongoing need to perform compactions. High compaction pressure means that there is a significant backlog of data that needs to be compacted, which may lead to increased resource usage and potentially affect read and write latencies.

(Measuring Average Response Time and P99)

  • Each DB is 100GB in allocated capacity, with an object size of 1K. 
  • On each of the 12 DBs we run a workload that contains 90% reads and only 10% overwrites. 
  • The tests ran on an Azure instance with: 
  • 16 CPU  
  • 128 GB 
  • 2 NVME SSD as a single partition

  2. With Speedb (vs RocksDB), the P99 is reduced by a factor of 3 to 4
  3. With Speedb (vs RocksDB), the average response time is reduced and nearly halved

SLA Based Capacity and Resource Planning

Speedb delivers stable and predictable performance also enables a first-time opportunity to do SLA based capacity and resource planning based on planned performance. 

RocksDB capacity and resource planning becomes exceedingly challenging, if not impossible, when dealing with unstable P99 (and P95) latency in relation to SLAs.

Considerations When Using RocksDB Instead of Speedb:

  • Unpredictable tail latency makes it difficult to anticipate and plan for high-latency outliers.
  • Unstable latency lacks consistent patterns, hindering the establishment of deterministic behavior for capacity planning.
  • Unreliable latency metrics lead to the risk of overprovisioning or underprovisioning resources.
  • Unstable latency necessitates a reactive approach to addressing performance issues instead of proactive planning.

Speedb has effectively resolved these issues, without any considerable trade-offs, which can be leveraged in Speedb’s latest Enterprise key-value storage engine version. 

Speedb Improves RocksDB Read Response Times and P99 Performance Instability 

By nature, multi-sharded environments are a challenge for any embedded storage engine library. What we have shown (again) is that myths and preconceptions are here to be broken. LSM trees can operate as best in-class performance for BOTH writes AND reads. 

By executing code level innovations and optimizations like tuned QoS controls on the background jobs, we not only stabilize the read performance and reduce the P99 by a factor that may reach 10x in certain cases, but also simultaneously reduce the average response time. 

When considering the impact on the application level performance, both P99 and average response time reductions are likely to have significantly positive effect on reads, with random, sequential and all forms of mixed workloads.

Conclusions

The root cause of read-related workload latency spikes is not the LSM-technology itself, but rather compaction related issues and the absence of advanced resource management (in RocksDB), and especially effective resource and QoS implementation and management.

Speedb has spent significant efforts addressing both of these issues and now shows significant benefits

  1. Reducing compaction overhead and WAF
  2. Implementing advanced resource management to improve overall performance and stabilize IOPS performance delivery, lower AND stabilize average response time and associated latencies.
  3. All of these improvements apply to sharded and non-sharded environments.
  4. Reduction in (related) associated CPU, memory and SSD resource consumption

These benefits translate into better front-end database and application and user related response times on mixed workloads and especially for read response times, finally shattering the myth that LSM tree and key-value store implementations are inherently limited for heavy read-oriented workloads. 

Consider Speedb Enterprise For Your Key-Value Store Deployments

Speedb, by improving read-performance will perform (at least) better than traditional B-Tree and LSM tree type performance and can be now be deployed for the heaviest of write AND read (and mixed workload) environments, making Speedb an excellent choice whenever a key-value storage engine can be utilized as the storage engine and other requirements fit.

Typical use-cases for Speedb include 100% compatible drop-in library replacement for RocksDB, but can be utilized by any key-value store appropriate environments.
